# YouTube Playlist Progress Trackers Compared, and How to Pick One

If you want to track a YouTube course and actually finish it, pick a tool by the gap you have. A length calculator solves planning. A checkbox extension solves visibility. Neither solves the return loop, which is the part that decides whether you finish. Most tools stop before that loop, and that omission is the single most useful thing to notice when choosing one.

YouTube itself provides none of this. No duration total, no completion state, no reminder, no progress view. Everything below exists to fill those gaps, and each tool fills a different subset.

## What a tracker actually needs to do

Evaluate any tool against five capabilities. Ordering matters, because the later ones are harder to build and more decisive for completion.

1. Length estimate. How long is this playlist in real time, not video count.
2. Finish-date planning. Runtime mapped against your daily pace.
3. Per-video completion. Check off what you watched, resume without hunting.
4. A return loop. Streaks, reminders, and visible progress that bring you back tomorrow.
5. Notes. Per-video capture so the effort compounds into a study artifact.

Capability four is where most tools stop short, and it is the one that maps to finishing rather than merely recording.

## The categories, and their trade-offs

### Playlist length calculators

Tools like ytplaylistlength and LenosTube do one job well. Paste a URL, get total runtime and watch time across playback speeds. They are stateless, which is their strength and their limit. No account, no friction, and no memory of your progress once you close the tab. Use one to plan, covered in [how long a playlist takes](/blog/how-long-does-it-take-to-finish-a-youtube-playlist), then move to something that persists state.

### Browser extensions

TrackMyCourse and similar extensions add checkboxes to the YouTube interface so you can mark videos watched in place. The advantage is context, you never leave the player. The trade-offs are real. Extension state usually lives in local browser storage, so it does not follow you to another device, and it disappears if you clear site data. There is no streak, no reminder, and no progress visualization, so the tool records completion without doing much to drive it.

### YouTube-to-course converters

YTCourse and comparable products restructure a playlist into a course-like layout with progress tracking and notes. This is a genuine step up if you want structure and a persistent account. The common gap is the daily accountability layer. Structure helps you organize the work. It does less to make you show up on a Tuesday when you would rather not.

### Paid course platforms

Udemy and its peers offer produced, structured content with tracking built in. If you would rather buy polished material than assemble free playlists, they are a reasonable choice. Two caveats. You leave YouTube's free library behind, and paying does not fix completion. Unfinished paid courses are common for the same structural reason free ones are, the absence of a return loop.

### Do-it-yourself in Notion or a spreadsheet

Maximum flexibility, maximum maintenance. A Notion tracker can do everything on the checklist if you build and maintain it, and the maintenance itself becomes a task people abandon. Reasonable if you already live in Notion, a trap if the tracker becomes a second project.

## How to choose

Match the tool to your actual gap rather than the longest feature list.

- You only need the runtime. Any length calculator.
- You want checkboxes inside YouTube and nothing more. A browser extension, accepting the single-device limit.
- You want structure and notes. A course converter such as YTCourse.
- Your real problem is not finishing. A tool with a return loop, such as PeakStreak.
- You prefer to pay for produced content. Udemy and peers, knowing completion is still on you.
